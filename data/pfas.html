Cross-State Intelligence

The PFAS legislative landscape as of March 2026 represents a coordinated, multi-front regulatory campaign reaching critical mass: 15 states simultaneously have bills past at least one chamber, with Virginia, Maryland, and Hawaii all in near-enactment posture within a compressed 30-45 day window. The Northeast corridor (NY, NJ, MD, MA, RI, VT, NH) is operating as an effective regulatory bloc with synchronized bill introductions, companion bill strategies, and shared model legislation from networks like Safer States, while the Midwest Great Lakes states (MN, WI, MI, IL) are developing parallel enforcement and remediation frameworks with industry exemption negotiations underway. For a US windows and doors manufacturer, the highest immediate risk is in product treatment coatings, fluoropolymer-based weatherstripping and gaskets, and PFAS-containing sealants or adhesives, all of which fall within the expanding 'intentionally added PFAS in products' regulatory scope advancing simultaneously in the 5-6 most active jurisdictions — compliance preparation should be underway now, not triggered by enactment.

Watch List
VA
Virginia has the most advanced PFAS bill pipeline of any state in the dataset with 9 bills at passed_one or advanced stages. At least 3 bills are in final pre-enactment posture. VA is the Southern state closest to enactment and will serve as the trigger for regional spread to NC, GA, and TN.
→ Pull the text of all 9 advanced VA bills immediately. Assess whether any address building products, treated materials, or construction components. Engage VA Building Industry Association to monitor amendment language before final votes. If products are affected, begin reformulation assessment and distributor notification protocol.
MD
Maryland passed 4 PFAS bills in 3 days (March 20-23) and has a hard session deadline in early April 2026. The cosmetic products enforcement bills could set penalty precedent applicable to other product categories. MD's aggressive enforcement posture (adding penalties to existing prohibitions) signals a move from prohibition to active enforcement phase.
→ Review MD's existing enacted PFAS product prohibitions for any construction material or treated product applicability. The shift to enforcement/penalty bills means prior 'prohibition' laws may now have teeth. Confirm product compliance with existing MD PFAS laws before enforcement provisions activate.
NY
New York has 48 total bills — second highest in the dataset — with 10 at passed_one stage covering drinking water MCLs, playground surfacing, and multiple other product categories. NY's legislative density and market size (largest US construction market) make any NY enactment disproportionately impactful. The playground surfacing ban is directly relevant to any manufacturer whose products contact outdoor surfaces.
→ Map all 10 passed_one NY bills to product lines. Specifically assess whether window and door framing materials, weatherstripping, or surface coatings fall under any bill's product scope. Retain NY regulatory counsel to monitor second-chamber amendments. Prepare compliance timeline if bills enact before December 2026.
MN
Minnesota has 60 total bills — the highest volume of any jurisdiction — with 58 in active committee markup. The two recent committee assignments on agricultural exemptions and appropriations modifications (2026-03-23) suggest active negotiation of a comprehensive PFAS framework. MN's 3M legacy means political pressure for strict standards is unusually high, and MN-enacted standards have historically been adopted by other states.
→ Monitor MN committee hearing schedules for any bills addressing manufactured goods or building products. MN has previously enacted expansive 'intentionally added PFAS' prohibitions; assess whether current bills expand scope to construction materials. Engage MN Chamber of Commerce for manufacturing sector representation in hearings.
NJ
New Jersey's apparel PFAS prohibition bill just passed one chamber on 2026-03-23 and NJ has a history of expanding product prohibitions beyond initial target categories through regulatory rulemaking. NJ's DEP has broad authority to designate additional PFAS-containing products as prohibited once a statutory framework exists.
→ Review NJ's apparel bill language for any definitional scope that could reach treated fabrics or polymer-based building components (e.g., weatherstripping, door seals). Engage NJ DEP regulatory affairs contacts to monitor any rulemaking notices triggered by new legislation. NJ enforcement is aggressive — compliance must precede enactment, not follow it.
WA
Washington has 2 bills at the 'advanced' stage and 12 total bills tracked. WA has already enacted multiple PFAS product laws and has regulatory infrastructure to enforce them. As the West Coast pacesetter, WA enactments directly influence OR and ID, and WA's compliance dates often set the practical West Coast compliance floor.
→ Identify the specific WA advanced bills and their effective dates. WA typically builds in 12-18 month compliance windows. If building products are covered, initiate supply chain PFAS disclosure requests to all direct material suppliers immediately. WA has active retail take-back and distributor liability provisions in prior laws that may apply.
HI
Hawaii passed 3 PFAS bills in 3 days covering environmental protection, wastewater, and single-use plastics. Hawaii's island geography creates unique disposal and contamination concerns that have historically led to stricter-than-mainland standards. HI enactments are watched closely by Pacific Rim trading partners and can influence federal EPA posture.
→ Review the single-use plastics PFAS bill for any language that could extend to packaging, product wrapping, or coated materials used in windows/doors shipments to HI. Assess Hawaii distribution chain for PFAS-containing packaging materials. HI's wastewater bill may affect manufacturing facility discharge compliance for any HI-based operations.
GA
Georgia has 6 bills in introduced status with none yet in committee — the pre-acceleration pattern. Georgia is the fastest-growing major construction market in the US (Atlanta metro led US housing permits in 2024-2025) and a major hub for windows/doors manufacturers and distributors. Early engagement now is far less costly than reactive compliance later.
→ Establish monitoring for when GA bills receive committee assignments — that is the trigger for active engagement. Identify GA legislative champions for construction industry interests. Begin pre-introduction of company representatives to GA Natural Resources and Environment committee staff. 2027 session preparation should start now.
